Subtheme: Speculation

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Is profit from flips driven more by renovation or market timing?
      In the MAPC report, "Homes for Profit," MAPC finds that investor flips resell for signficantly more than non investors. Oftentimes, flips are framed as just "greedy profits." Thus, I'm curious as to how much of the resale premium comes from market wide price movement (market timing/greedy profit) in comparison to property specific value addition (renovation).
  2. How has corporate ownership growth accelerated after major economic shocks, for example such as 2008 and 2020?
      The ProPublica article, "When Private Equity Becomes Your Landlord," mentions how private equity expanded aggressively after the Great Recession, taking advantage of crisis for the common people as an opportunity to acquire assets. However, I'm very curious of the extent to which corporations acquire after economic crisis compared to regular homeowners. This is because anecdotally, my single mother was actually only able to afford our home after the 2008 economic crisis. Thus, I'm wondering how much my personal experience diverges from the average.
  3. Do corporations purchase homes in neighborhoods before major price appreciation or after?
      Both the MAPC and ProPublica articles describe corporate investors as active actors looking to seek high returns rather than passive participants that respond to price increases. In other words, if corporations purchase homes before major price appreciation, this implies forward looking speculation. Otherwise, corporations may just be following the market. Thus, this question targets whether speculation is directly shaping the market or just simply responding to it.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

The data suggests that while the narratives presented in the articles contain elements of truth, the reality of Boston's housing market is more nuanced. For example, institutional flip profits do appear to be driven more by broader market timing than by substantial renovation driven value addition. In opposition, the narrative around a dramatic shift in corporate ownership starting from the 2008 recession is more fuzzy. While corporate ownership has steadily increased over time, there is little evidence of a dramatic post 2008 takeover. Instead, 2020 marks a more noticeable inflection point. Additionally, corporate ownership growth does not consistently precede price appreciation, weakening the argument that corporations systematically drive speculative price booms. Overall, the trends point less to sudden crisis driven takeovers and more to a gradual structural shift in ownership patterns shaped by broader macroeconomic factors.
